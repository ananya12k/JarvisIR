3) What the numbers mean
- Volumetric blur and heterogeneous density appear in almost every top configuration. This suggests they are the most important degradations in this ablation.
- Secondary scattering also helps strongly when combined with volumetric blur and heterogeneous density.
- Fog-coupled noise improves the overall ranking in several cases, but it does not always produce the highest final PSNR/SSIM by itself.
- Nonlinear gamma helps, but it is usually less dominant than volumetric blur, heterogeneous density, and secondary scattering.
- Adaptive airlight is useful for raising final restored PSNR/SSIM, especially in the strongest multi-factor combinations.

4) Single-degradation observations
Among the single degradation settings:
- combo_volumetric_blur gives the best restored PSNR among single-factor settings (13.390082) and the best restored SSIM (0.883441).
- combo_adaptive_airlight is close behind in restored PSNR (13.361957) and SSIM (0.874884).
- combo_secondary_scattering and combo_nonlinear_gamma are weaker in final restored quality than volumetric blur or adaptive airlight.
- combo_fog_coupled_noise is useful, but by itself it is not as strong as the best single-factor options.

5) Recommended conclusion
The ablation indicates that the restoration pipeline performs best when it handles coupled structural fog effects first, especially volumetric blur and heterogeneous density. Secondary scattering is the next most valuable factor, while fog-coupled noise and nonlinear gamma provide additional but smaller gains. Adaptive airlight is most effective when combined with the stronger fog components, where it improves final restored quality.

Final conclusion: the most reliable overall configuration for this fog ablation is the combination of volumetric blur + heterogeneous density + secondary scattering + fog-coupled noise, while the highest final restoration quality is achieved when adaptive airlight is also included in the strongest multi-factor setting.
